## What accounts for roughly 90% of all deck collapses?

Three primary structural failures account for the vast majority of deck collapses:

**Ledger board failure or improper attachment to the house:** The ledger board is the horizontal framing member that anchors your deck to your home's structure. When this connection fails, whether from undersized lag bolts, missing flashing, or rotted rim joists, the entire deck can pull away from the house. For **Bozeman remodeling** projects involving new or replacement **decks Bozeman**, **Heartwood Craft** uses through-bolted connections with proper spacing, stainless steel hardware, and comprehensive flashing systems to prevent water infiltration.

**Rot or corrosion in posts, joists, and fasteners:** Bozeman's moisture from snow melt and rain, combined with freeze-thaw cycles, accelerates rot in untreated or improperly treated lumber. Galvanized fasteners rust out, joist hangers corrode, and structural members weaken invisibly inside the deck structure. Regular inspections catch these issues before they become dangerous.

**Poor footing installation or unstable soil:** Posts must be anchored to concrete footings that extend below the frost line, 48 inches in Bozeman. Shallow footings heave during freeze-thaw cycles, compromising stability. ## What is the 3/4/5 rule for decks?

The 3/4/5 rule is a simple but essential framing technique to ensure your deck corners are perfectly square, which is critical for even load distribution and structural integrity. Here's how it works:

- Measure 3 feet along one edge of your deck frame

- Measure 4 feet along the perpendicular edge

- Measure the diagonal between these two points

- If the diagonal measures exactly 5 feet, your corner is square (90 degrees)

This principle comes from the Pythagorean theorem (3² + 4² = 5²) and can be scaled up, 6/8/10, 9/12/15, etc., for larger decks. Square corners ensure that joists align properly, loads distribute evenly, and the deck structure performs as engineered.

## Is a deck collapse covered by homeowners insurance?

Coverage depends on the cause of collapse and your policy specifics:

**Typically covered:** Collapses caused by sudden or accidental events, such as structural failure during normal use, storm damage, or unexpected material defects. If your properly maintained deck fails due to a hidden defect or sudden structural issue, most homeowners policies will cover repairs and liability.

**Typically not covered:** Damage caused by neglect, deferred maintenance, or gradual deterioration. If an inspection would have revealed rot, corroded fasteners, or structural weakness that you failed to address, your insurer may deny the claim.

**Liability considerations:** If someone is injured during a deck collapse, your homeowner's liability coverage should protect you, but only if you can demonstrate the deck was properly maintained and inspected. Documentation matters.
