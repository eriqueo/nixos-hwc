**The danger zone is Weeks 1--3.** You've spent the deposit on materials
and labor but haven't hit the next milestone yet. If you're running one
job at a time with no overlap, you're living on that \$1,500--\$4,500
cushion for 2--3 weeks. If the client delays a selection or something
goes wrong, that cushion disappears.

Payment Structure That Protects You

Your current milestone structure through JT/Stripe is good. Here's the
optimized version:

- **Deposit: 30% at contract signing.** This must cover all material
  orders plus 1--2 weeks of labor. Never start a job where the deposit
  doesn't cover your material outlay.

- **Milestone 1: 20% at rough-in complete.** Framing, plumbing rough,
  electrical rough done. Client can see the bones of the project. This
  payment refills your cash position before the expensive tile/finish
  phase.

- **Milestone 2: 40% at substantial completion.** Tile, paint, vanity,
  fixtures installed. The bathroom looks done even if punch items
  remain. This is your largest payment because it's when the client sees
  the transformation.

- **Final: 10% at final walkthrough.** Punch list resolved, everything
  clean. Small enough that you're not financially dependent on it, but
  large enough that the client has incentive to do the walkthrough
  promptly.

The Cash Flow Buffer Rule

Keep a minimum of \$10,000 in your business account at all times. This
is your buffer for: material orders on the next job before the deposit
clears, unexpected costs on the current job, the 2--4 week gap between
finishing one job and starting the next, and quarterly tax payments. If
your balance drops below \$10K, that's a red flag that means either your
payment milestones are wrong, your margins are too thin, or you're
spending on overhead you can't afford yet.

3\. Pricing Model: Markup vs. Margin

Your estimator uses a cost × markup model. It's critical you understand
the difference between markup and margin, because confusing them is the
#1 way contractors lose money.

  -------------- -------------- -------------- ------------ --------------
  **Cost**       **Markup %**   **Price**      **Margin %** **Profit**

  \$100          25%            \$125          20%          \$25

  \$100          33%            \$133          25%          \$33

  \$100          43%            \$143          30%          \$43

  \$100          50%            \$150          33%          \$50

  \$100          67%            \$167          40%          \$67
  -------------- -------------- -------------- ------------ --------------

**Your Dempsey job used a \~33% markup (cost × 1.333 = price), which
yields a 25% margin on most items.** Your newer estimates from the
assembler use a \~43% markup (cost × 1.43), which yields 30% margin. The
higher rate is correct --- 30% is the minimum margin you should accept
on any job. Here's why:

- **Below 25% margin:** You're essentially working at cost once overhead
  is included. Any surprise (material price increase, extra demo day,
  callback) puts you in the red on that job.

- **25--30% margin:** Survivable but tight. This is where most
  remodeling contractors operate. Leaves some room for error but not
  enough to build cash reserves.

- **30--35% margin:** Healthy. This is your target. Absorbs surprises,
  funds overhead, and leaves a livable owner's take. Your estimator is
  calibrated for this range.

- **35%+ margin:** Premium positioning. Achievable if you're
  differentiated (3D renderings, exceptional communication, specialized
  expertise). Don't chase this by padding estimates --- earn it by
  delivering more value.

6\. Taxes: Single-Member LLC in Montana

As a single-member LLC, your business income passes through to your
personal return. You pay income tax + self-employment tax on net profit.
Montana has no sales tax, which simplifies things.

What You Owe (Estimated)

- **Self-employment tax: 15.3%** on net profit (Social Security 12.4% +
  Medicare 2.9%). This is on top of income tax. It's the biggest
  surprise for new business owners.

- **Federal income tax: 10--22%** depending on your total household
  income. At \$18K net profit, you're likely in the 12% bracket.

- **Montana state income tax: 4.7--6.75%** (Montana uses a graduated
  rate structure).

**At \$18K net profit, rough tax estimate:** \$2,754 SE tax + \$2,160
federal + \$1,000 state = \~\$5,900. That leaves \$12,100 actual
take-home from \$150K revenue. This is why margins and volume both
matter so much.

Quarterly Estimated Payments

You're required to pay estimated taxes quarterly if you'll owe more than
\$1,000 for the year. Deadlines:

- Q1: April 15

- Q2: June 15

- Q3: September 15

- Q4: January 15 (of next year)

**The simple approach:** Set aside 30% of every dollar of net profit
(revenue minus expenses) into a separate savings account. Make quarterly
payments from that account. If you overpay, you get a refund. If you
underpay, you owe penalties. The 30% rule covers SE tax + income tax
with a small buffer.

Deductions You Should Be Tracking

- **Vehicle:** Standard mileage rate (\$0.70/mile in 2026) or actual
  expenses. Track every business mile. Use an app like MileIQ or just a
  notes file.

- **Home office:** If you use part of your home exclusively for business
  (your NixOS homeserver room counts), you can deduct the proportional
  share of rent/mortgage, utilities, internet.

- **Tools and equipment:** Everything you buy for work. Section 179
  allows full expensing of equipment purchases up to \$1.22M.

- **Software subscriptions:** JT, QB, Tailscale, n8n hosting costs,
  domain hosting, etc.

- **Insurance premiums:** GL, workers comp, and health insurance
  (deductible on Schedule 1 as self-employed).

- **Marketing:** LSA spend, print materials, chamber dues, any
  advertising.

- **Continuing education:** Courses, certifications, books related to
  your trade.

- **QBI deduction:** As a sole proprietor, you may qualify for a 20%
  deduction on qualified business income. This is significant --- at
  \$18K net profit, it's potentially \$3,600 off your taxable income.

- You're consistently booking 2+ months ahead

- You're turning away or delaying 1+ qualified leads per month

- Your trailing 6-month revenue averages \$20K+/month (\$240K

current volume, subs make more sense than employees. Start building sub
relationships now so they're available when you need them.

8\. 3D Renderings: Turning a Differentiator Into Revenue

You do real 3D renderings. Most contractors in Bozeman can't. This is a
competitive advantage, but right now it's being given away for free
during the estimating process. That needs to change.

The Design Fee Model

Offer 3D visualization as a paid pre-construction service. This solves
two problems: it stops you from doing free design work for clients who
may not sign, and it creates a revenue stream that's not tied to
construction.

- **Price: \$500--\$1,000** depending on complexity. A single bathroom
  with 2--3 options = \$500. A full bathroom with multiple layout
  alternatives = \$1,000.

- **Credit toward project:** If the client signs a construction
  contract, the design fee is credited toward the project total. They're
  not paying twice --- they're paying a deposit that happens to come
  with a 3D preview.

- **Keeps the design if they don't sign:** If they choose another
  contractor, they keep the renderings and you keep the \$500--\$1,000.
  Your time was compensated.

- **Marketing asset:** Every rendering you create (with permission)
  becomes portfolio content for your website's empty "Our Work" page.
  Before/after + rendering creates a powerful three-image story.

**This is the design resource gate from your client journey funnel.**
When a client in Stage 3 (Discover) needs design help: "I offer a 3D
design visualization for \$500--\$1,000 that gets credited toward the
project if you move forward. You'll see exactly what your bathroom will
look like before any construction starts." This positions you as
premium, protects your time, and gives the client something tangible
that makes them more likely to commit.
