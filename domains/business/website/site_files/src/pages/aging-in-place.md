## This Page Is a Resource, Not a Sales Pitch

I'm writing this because I keep having the same conversation with homeowners — usually in their 50s or 60s — who love their home but are starting to wonder how long it'll work for them. The bathtub is getting harder to step over. The hallway to the bedroom feels narrower every year. The lighting isn't what it used to be.

They don't want to move. They don't want a nursing home. They want to stay where they are, in the house where they raised their kids and know every neighbor's name. That's a reasonable thing to want, and with the right modifications, most homes can support it.

This page explains what universal design is, what the most common modifications look like, and how to think about timing and cost. If you want to talk about your specific situation after reading, I'm here. But the information comes first.

## What Universal Design Actually Means

Universal design isn't about making your home look like a medical facility. It's the opposite.

The concept was developed by architect Ron Mace at North Carolina State University. His argument was simple: good design should work for the widest range of people without needing specialized adaptations. A curbless shower isn't a disability feature — it's a better shower. A lever door handle isn't an accommodation — it's easier for everyone, including the person carrying groceries.

The best universal design is invisible. You walk into a bathroom and think "this is beautiful." You don't notice that the shower has no curb, the grab bars match the fixtures, and the vanity height works whether you're standing or seated. That's the point.

Here in Bozeman, this matters more than you might think. Montana's population is aging — and most of us plan to stay. The question isn't whether your home will need to adapt. It's whether you adapt it now, on your terms, or later, under pressure.

## Where It Matters Most

### Bathrooms

This is the #1 aging-in-place project, and for good reason. About 80% of falls among older adults happen in the bathroom. The fixes are well-understood:

**Curbless showers.** Zero-threshold entry — no lip to step over. These are a premium design feature in high-end homes. They just happen to also be wheelchair-accessible. I install linear drains with proper slope so water stays where it belongs.

**Grab bars that don't look like grab bars.** Modern grab bars come in brushed gold, matte black, oil-rubbed bronze — they look like designer towel bars. I also install blocking behind tile during every bathroom remodel, so if you don't want bars now, adding them later is a 30-minute job instead of a tile demolition.

**Comfort-height toilets and vanities.** Standard toilet height is 15 inches. Comfort height is 17-19 inches. That's the difference between struggling to stand and standing easily. Same principle applies to vanity height.

**Non-slip flooring.** Textured porcelain tile rated for wet areas. Looks great, doesn't get slippery when wet.

**Hand-held showerheads on slide bars.** Useful at every age, essential as mobility changes.

If you're already thinking about a [bathroom remodel](/bathroom/remodeling/), adding these features costs very little extra when the walls are already open. That's the key insight most people miss.

### Doorways and Hallways

Standard interior doors are 30 inches wide. A wheelchair needs 36 inches minimum. Even without a wheelchair, wider doorways make daily life easier — moving furniture, carrying laundry, walking side by side with someone who needs support.

Widening a doorway during a remodel adds a few hundred dollars. Doing it after everything is finished? That's thousands, because you're ripping out trim, patching drywall, and repainting.

### First-Floor Living

Stairs become the biggest barrier as mobility changes. If your home's only bedroom and full bathroom are on the second floor, that's eventually a problem.

Converting a main-floor room into a bedroom suite — or adding a full bathroom to the first floor — means you can live comfortably on one level. This is a significant project, but it's the modification that most often keeps people in their homes rather than moving to a single-story.

### Lighting and Electrical

Vision changes with age, and dim hallways cause falls. The fixes:

- Task lighting under cabinets and in closets
- Motion-sensor night lights in hallways and bathrooms
- Rocker-style light switches at accessible heights (easier than toggle switches)
- Brighter overall illumination with layered lighting design

### Kitchen Accessibility

- Pull-out shelves and drawers instead of fixed shelving
- D-style cabinet pulls (easier to grip than knobs)
- Lever-handle faucets
- Varied-height countertops for seated and standing use
- Wall ovens instead of reaching into low ranges

### Exterior and Entry

- No-step entries or ramps that blend with your home's design
- Lever door hardware
- Well-lit pathways and covered entries
- Slip-resistant deck surfaces

In Montana, this also means thinking about ice management. A covered entry with proper drainage and heating elements in the walkway isn't luxury — it's fall prevention.

## The Best Time to Plan Is Before You Need It

Here's the thing most people don't realize: the most cost-effective time to add universal design features is during a remodel you're already planning.

Adding blocking behind tile for future grab bars costs almost nothing when the walls are open. Widening a doorway during a renovation is a minor line item. Installing a curbless shower instead of a standard one adds modest cost to a bathroom remodel that's already happening.

Doing all of this retroactively — after the tile is set, the trim is installed, and the paint is dry — costs dramatically more.

I encourage every homeowner, regardless of age, to think about universal design during any remodel. You're not planning for disability. You're planning for life. A knee surgery. A broken ankle. A parent who comes to stay. A toddler learning to walk. These features help everyone, every day.

## "Aging in Place" vs. Universal Design

You'll see both terms used interchangeably, but there's a meaningful distinction.

"Aging in place" focuses specifically on modifications for older adults staying in their homes. It's reactive — there's a need, and you address it.

"Universal design" is broader. It's about creating spaces that work for everyone at every stage of life, from the start. It's proactive — you build flexibility into the home so it adapts with you.

I think the universal design framing is more honest and more useful. Nobody wants to feel like they're admitting something is wrong. But everyone wants a home that works better.

## What Does It Cost?

Every project is different, but here are realistic ranges for Bozeman:

- **Adding grab bars and hand-held shower** (minimal renovation): **$500 – $2,000**
- **Curbless shower conversion** (existing bathroom): **$8,000 – $18,000**
- **Full accessible bathroom remodel**: **$18,000 – $45,000+**
- **Doorway widening** (per opening): **$300 – $1,500**
- **First-floor bathroom addition**: **$25,000 – $50,000+**
- **Comprehensive whole-home modifications**: **$40,000 – $80,000+**

The range depends on scope, finishes, and existing conditions. An itemized estimate shows you exactly where every dollar goes — no surprises.

**Want a starting point?** Try the [bathroom cost calculator](/calculator/) to get a ballpark for the bathroom portion.

## Are There Grants or Tax Benefits?

In some cases, yes:

- **Medical expense deductions** may apply if modifications are prescribed by a physician.
- **VA Specially Adapted Housing grants** are available for eligible veterans — these can be substantial.
- **Montana property tax assistance** programs exist for qualifying seniors.
- **Some long-term care insurance policies** cover home modifications.

I'd recommend checking with your tax advisor for specifics. I can point you toward the right resources if you're not sure where to start.
